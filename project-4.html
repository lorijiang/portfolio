Project Brief

In this academic project, teams collaborated with clients to address interaction design issues. Our team partnered with Heritage Burnaby to regenerate interest in their online map, Charting Changes Atlas.

The Client

Who are Heritage Burnaby?

Heritage Burnaby is an online repository for Burnaby's historical archives. They have an online interactive map called the Charting Changes Atlas which was created in 2011 as a digital museum of Burnaby's landmarks tracked over time.

The Problem Space

What is Charting Changes Atlas?

Charting Changing Atlas is an online interactive map launched by Heritage Burnaby in 2011, and has not been updated since then. Our client wanted to know how to regenerate interest in the map in 2023 with a fresh target user group.

Problem

People don't feel personally motivated to engage with the map.

User research revealed that the map lacked purpose, felt impersonal, and had major usability issues like unintuitive navigation, poor information organization, and unappealing visual design. This stemmed from the map's outdated purpose and audience from 2011, leading us with the challenge of re-aligning the map to current interests.

Solution

Repurpose the map as a story map of community-driven photos and stories.

In order to address this lack of personal connection, we redesigned the map to integrate personalized storytelling, by allowing Burnaby residents to share their stories and photos connected to historical landmarks on the map. We also fixed major usability issues to improve user engagement and retention.

User Research

Method

Two methods, usability testing with think-aloud and semi-structured interviews, were employed. Eight participants, recruited through snowball sampling, included Burnaby residents, history workers or students, and enthusiasts. They explored the interactive map while our team recorded their verbalized thoughts, and were interviewed after about their attitudes towards it.

Main insights from usability testing

Main insights from semi-structured interview

Using affinity diagramming in Miro we sorted the positive and negative comments from the interview into themes, leading to these main insights:

Positive

  • photos helped make map more interesting
  • informative text and photos
  • interactive way to learn about history

    • Negative

  • map lacked purpose
  • content felt impersonal and irrelevant
  • design felt outdated and unappealing
  • frustrated by disorganized information and difficult navigation

    • Narrowing Down the Problem

    Synthesizing Research into Major Pain Points

    1) Map lacks clear purpose

    2) Map feels impersonal, preventing users from wanting to interact

    3) Poor navigation and information organization prevent usability

    4) Outdated visual design makes website feel unappealing

    Define

    Problem Statement

    How can we improve usability and personalize the interactive map while retaining it's historical/informational value to increase people's interest and engagement with the map?

    Ideate

    Participatory Workshop to Brainstorm Solutions

    Who

    Our team, the client, 4 users who fit our personas.

    How

    Online, hosted on Miro and Zoom.

    What

    1 hour workshop with 3 brainstorming activities.

    Why

    Involving users in our brainstorming sessions allowed us to bounce ideas off each other, while also getting real-time feedback on their attitudes towards design decisions.

    Proposed Solution

    Repurpose the interactive map into a community-driven storymap , integrating local stories. This maintains historical information in a separate tab, ensuring relevance to users while fostering community engagement.

    Ideate

    Competitive Analysis

    I looked at other map-based interfaces for inspiration and found the webpages for Layers of London and Airbnb to have the most relevant UI design elements to apply to our story map.

    Key insights

  • full screen map with minimizable side panel helps to optimize screen space
  • multiple ways to navigate information (side panel suggestions, targeted search, and free discovery on map) gives users more freedom and control.
  • organizing map pinpoints in clusters helps reduce cognitive load

    Key Design Decisions

    Design System

    I created a design system to ensure cohesion when working as a team on the prototype, and also allow for consistency in future content updates.

    Key Design Decisions

    Addressing Usability Issues

  • new design system for cohesion with rest of website
  • multiple ways to navigate information
  • reducing cognitive load from three to two columns
  • full-bleed image displays to maximize screen space
  • chunking pinpoints to reduce visual clutter

    • Key Design Decisions

    Community Stories

  • new landing page community-driven by personalized storytelling submissions so that users are personally motivated to interact with it
  • renaming 'Charting Changes Atlas' to 'Story Map' to align with repurposed map and reduce confusion

    • Key Design Decisions

    Story Submission

  • word count and photo limit for short, impactful stories
  • separate pages for adding photo files and choosing thumbnails to reduce cognitive load

    • Key Design Decisions

    Historical Information

  • keeping positively viewed aspects of the original map
  • slider for more control over date range
  • detailed information appears on side panel rather than as pop-up

    • Reflection

    What I learned

    The design process is iterative, requiring us to constantly reassess and refine our problem statement. Throughout multiple iterations, we prioritized decisions that closely matched our users' needs. This experience taught me the importance of remaining flexible as a designer, willing to adapt and modify design choices in pursuit of project objectives.

    Things that can be improved

    Due to the project's scope and time limitations, we couldn't carry out usability testing to validate our design. However, conducting usability testing would be my next step to further develop this project.
